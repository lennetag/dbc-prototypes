When you need a digital business case

This guidance applies to digital, data and technology (DDaT) spend only. It is not general guidance for non‑digital spending.

When you need approval

You should write a business case when you need approval to:

  • start a new digital product or service
  • make a significant change to a live digital service (including improving reliability, performance, accessibility or security)
  • buy digital technology or digital services (including major contract changes)
  • commit to a funding envelope for a digital portfolio
  • increase spend, extend scope, or move into a new phase where the risk, cost or commitment changes

A business case should be proportionate to the scale and risk of what you are proposing. It should set out what you will deliver, how you will measure success, and why this is value for money.

Types of business case you can use

For digital spend, you may be asked for one of the following, depending on what you are trying to do.

  • Model A: staged funding for uncertain work — use when there is meaningful uncertainty at the start. Funding is released in stages, based on evidence of progress.
  • Model B: staged funding for live services — use for services that are already live and need ongoing run and change funding. Decisions are linked to agreed outcome metrics and regular performance reviews.
  • Model C: portfolio outcome‑based funding — use when you want a single multi‑year business case for a portfolio of related digital capabilities, with flexibility to move funding between projects within an agreed envelope.

Adopting a model in your department

Once a department has agreed with their HMT Spending Team to explore a pathfinder model, the focus shifts inward — to preparing the right environment to operate under a different funding arrangement. These sections set out what that looks like in practice.

Central support is there — trust it. HMT Spending Teams, GDS and NISTA will support departments throughout the pathfinder process. Departments do not need to build new governance infrastructure from scratch or develop internal guidance in isolation. The support package is designed to do the heavy lifting centrally, so departments can focus on delivery.

Internal conversations to have

Before formally engaging with the pathfinder process, departments should ensure the following internal conversations have taken place.

Conversation What to cover
HMT Spending Team Confirm suitability of the model, agree the scope of the initiative, and understand what HMT will need to see at each review point. This conversation should happen before anything else.
Strategic Finance Lead (or equivalent) Ensure the senior finance function understands what the model is for and how it works. There are implications for budget management, reporting lines, and in‑year flexibility. Agreement avoids complications at the formal adoption point.
CDIO / Digital Lead Confirm the team, tooling and data infrastructure are in place to support whatever reporting and outcome measurements are required.
SRO (if different from the above) Ensure accountability for the programme and understanding of the value and risks of staged funding — particularly for Model A — and be comfortable with the possibility of early stopping if an approach is not delivering.
Internal Assurance / Finance Business Partner Confirm internal assurance teams understand HMT requirements. Early agreement avoids duplication and ensures the department is ready to provide the new model’s assurance in parallel with it.

Ongoing governance once a pathfinder is running

Once a pathfinder is approved and underway, the ongoing governance is deliberately lighter than traditional processes. The rhythm and format will vary by model, but the following principles apply across all three:

  • Reviews are evidence‑led, not documentation‑led. Teams present data on outcomes and progress — not lengthy written reports.
  • Central reviewers (GDS, HMT, NISTA) are there to support, not just scrutinise. The review process is designed to help teams succeed, not catch them out.
  • Internal strategic finance teams should be kept informed at each review cycle — not just at formal approval points. Regular touchpoints prevent surprises.
  • Escalation routes are clear. If something material changes — scope, risk, team, funding pressure — flag it to your HMT Spending Team early, not at the next scheduled review.

When things don’t go to plan

One of the most important features of these funding models — particularly Model A — is that they are designed to make it easier to stop as well as start. This is a feature, not a limitation.

The core ambition of the pathfinder programme is financial agility: the ability to pursue promising initiatives earlier and more easily, and to redirect resources quickly when something is not working. A programme that is evaluated rigorously and stopped at the right moment is a successful outcome — it has saved money, generated learning, and freed up budget for something better.

Failure is part of the design.

The upside of stopping: When a Model A pathfinder is stopped because progress cannot be demonstrated, the funding does not disappear — it is available to be redirected to other initiatives. The ability to start things more easily is only valuable if you are allowed to stop them when the evidence says to.

Evaluated failure is good: An initiative stopped after rigorous evaluation at stage 2 is a better outcome than a traditional programme that runs for three years before anyone acknowledges it is not working.

This requires cultural readiness: Departments need SROs and finance leads who are comfortable with this framing. Stopping should not be seen as a governance failure — it should be recognised as the model working as intended.

Formal exit and escalation

If a pathfinder needs to be stopped, scaled back, or escalated, the following applies.

Scenario What happens
Planned stopping (Model A) At any stage gate, the department and GDS/HMT agree that progress has not been evidenced. Funding ceases for that initiative. The department notifies its internal assurance function and Strategic Finance Lead. No new business case is required — the stopping decision is documented through the existing review record.
Underperformance (Model B) If performance metrics fall below agreed thresholds, an additional review is triggered. The team can explain and propose a remediation plan. If performance does not recover within an agreed timeframe, funding may be revised downward or the model exited — returning to a standard funding arrangement.
Portfolio rebalancing (Model C) If an individual project within a portfolio is stopped, its budget is available for reallocation within the portfolio envelope — without a new business case. If the portfolio as a whole is underperforming against its outcome framework, a formal portfolio review is triggered with HMT.
Escalation at any point If the department identifies a material risk — financial, delivery, or reputational — that was not anticipated at approval, it should escalate immediately to its HMT Spending Team. Earlier escalation is better than late disclosure.
